# UK Retail and Commercial Banking Industry Foundation — New Build

## 1. Delivery and input manifest

**Run mode:** New Build.
**Industry type:** Regulated commercial and public-interest financial-services industry, composed of regulated balance-sheet banks, payment infrastructures, technology and data dependencies, fintech participants, public institutions and specialist service providers.
**Effective date:** 17 July 2026.
**Source boundary:** Public-domain evidence only. No Banking Reinvention Blueprint, Founder Design Knowledge, Provider Fit, relationship intelligence or private commercial information used.
**Decision purpose:** Establish an independent current-state model of how UK retail and commercial banking works today, before future-state design, Transformation Gap analysis or Opportunity Twin creation.
**Accountability horizon:** 2026–2028, with special attention to regulation, resilience, AI/cloud, open banking, payments, fraud and SME/customer economics.
**Materiality rule:** Include entities, pressures, mechanisms and suppliers that materially affect customer access, deposits, lending, payments, risk, regulation, operating resilience, economics, technology dependencies or transformation capacity.
**Exclusions:** Insurance, wealth management, pensions, investment banking, trading/capital markets, asset management and crypto are excluded except where they affect retail/commercial banking distribution, payments, customer relationships, operational resilience or regulatory pressure.
**Method note:** The uploaded CIOS Researcher pack is used as operating method only, not as banking evidence.

## 2. Executive Industry Brief

The UK retail and commercial banking industry is not just a set of banks. It is a regulated operating system for household money, SME and business finance, deposits, credit, payments, identity, fraud control, conduct, liquidity, capital and operational resilience. The CMA’s retail banking market investigation explicitly covered personal current accounts and banking services to small and medium-sized enterprises, and its remedies focused on open banking, overdraft transparency, business-account opening and service-quality publication, which supports treating retail and SME/commercial banking as linked but distinct markets. ([GOV.UK][1])

The economic centre of gravity remains deposit-taking banks with current-account relationships, lending books, capital requirements and liquidity obligations. The FCA’s strategic review found that personal current accounts create competitive advantage for major banks, while challenger and digital banks gained share in personal and business current accounts; it also found intensified competition in mortgage and consumer-credit markets. ([FCA][2])

The customer model is splitting. Many customers are moving decisively to digital banking and app-led service, but vulnerable customers, branch-dependent customers, cash users and some small businesses still need physical or assisted access. Financial Lives 2024 was based on 17,950 UK adults and covers payments, mortgages, credit, retail banking, vulnerability and consumer trust; its published findings include material low resilience and vulnerability, making financial inclusion a core industry mechanism rather than a side issue. ([FCA][3])

The infrastructure layer is becoming strategically visible. CHAPS handles a tiny share of payment volume but the overwhelming majority of sterling payment value; open banking connections, API calls and payments are growing quickly; APP fraud reimbursement, Confirmation of Payee and operational-resilience rules are turning payments, data and fraud controls into regulated industry-wide operating mechanisms. ([Bank of England][4])

The banking technology stack is now a supervisory concern. In July 2026, HM Treasury designated Microsoft, Google Cloud, AWS and Oracle as critical third parties for UK financial services, giving the Bank of England, PRA and FCA direct oversight powers over systemic technology services. That makes cloud, AI, data and outsourcing dependencies part of the industry’s regulated operating model, not merely bank-by-bank IT choices. ([GOV.UK][5])

Current economics are strong at system level but uneven by participant type. The Bank of England’s July 2026 Financial Stability Report said the UK banking system was appropriately capitalised and had high liquidity; major UK banks had an aggregate CET1 ratio of 14.2%, liquidity coverage ratio of 142% and aggregate RoTE of 15.3% in Q1 2026. At the same time, SME supply frictions, fraud, technology failures, branch closures and customer trust deficits show that profitability does not equal full customer-value resolution. ([Bank of England][6])

## 3. Industry Boundary

### Included core boundary

**Retail banking** includes personal current accounts, savings, overdrafts, mortgages, credit cards, unsecured personal lending, cash access, branch and digital servicing, customer onboarding, complaints, fraud protection and retail conduct obligations. Retail banking is included because it anchors deposits, daily money movement, credit access, household financial resilience and trust.

**Business and commercial banking** includes business current accounts, SME lending, overdrafts, asset finance, invoice finance, working-capital finance, cards, merchant and cash-management services, relationship banking, payments, onboarding, KYC/KYB, fraud and financial-crime controls. It is included because SME finance and transaction banking connect customer value, deposits, risk, data, productivity and regional economic activity. The British Business Bank reported that gross SME bank lending reached £68bn in 2025 and that challenger and specialist banks accounted for 60% of gross SME bank lending, showing commercial banking is no longer a purely incumbent-bank domain. ([British Business Bank][7])

**Corporate banking** is included only where relevant to shared commercial-banking platforms, transaction banking, deposits, cash management, payments, financial-crime controls, risk, liquidity, relationship models or banking infrastructure. It is not treated as investment banking.

**Payments and banking infrastructure** are included because UK banking value is carried through CHAPS, Faster Payments, Bacs, direct debits, cards, open banking APIs, Confirmation of Payee, current-account switching, ATMs, cash access, LINK, the Post Office network and banking hubs. CHAPS value and open-banking growth show that banking cannot be explained from bank product P&Ls alone. ([Bank of England][4])

**Digital-native challengers** are included where they hold banking permissions or provide current-account, business-account, savings, lending or payments services that alter acquisition, service, deposits, data, pricing, switching or customer expectations. Monzo and Starling are material because both now report scale, deposits and profitability or near-scale economics rather than only product experimentation. ([Monzo][8])

**Adjacent fintechs** are included when they shape banking journeys, payments, lending, open banking, embedded finance, merchant services, fraud, identity, data, accounting, credit decisioning or customer acquisition. They are not all “banks”; many operate as infrastructure, distribution, data, lending, compliance or payment participants.

### Excluded or peripheral

Insurance, investment banking, capital markets, wealth management, pensions, asset management and trading are excluded unless they directly affect a retail/commercial banking customer journey, branch/app distribution, balance-sheet funding, commercial relationship, operational resilience or regulatory pressure.

## 4. Industry Value System

**How customer value is created:** customers receive safe storage of money, access to payment rails, credit, cash, identity assurance, fraud protection, business working capital, relationship support and regulated dispute resolution. Customer value is created when banks and infrastructure participants make money usable, movable, borrowable and protected.

**How enterprises generate revenue:** banks earn net interest income from lending and deposit funding, fees from current-account, card, payment, merchant, commercial and service activity, and ancillary income from related products. Digital challengers add subscription, interchange, lending, treasury and marketplace-style economics, but still depend on deposits, lending discipline, compliance and customer primacy. Monzo reported FY2026 revenue of £1.7bn, deposits of £25.7bn and 15.2m customers; Starling reported FY2025 revenue of £714m, deposits of £12.1bn and profit before tax of £223m. ([Monzo][8])

**Where principal costs arise:** people, branches, contact centres, technology, cloud, data, cyber, compliance, fraud losses and reimbursement, financial-crime controls, payment processing, complaints, credit impairments, property, outsourcing, capital and liquidity. Fraud is a direct cost and a trust mechanism: UK Finance reported £629.3m stolen through fraud in H1 2025 and £870m of unauthorised fraud prevented by banks and card companies. ([UK Finance][9])

**How capital, risk and regulation shape economics:** capital and liquidity buffers constrain balance-sheet leverage but protect confidence; credit risk changes impairment and pricing; conduct rules shape product design and servicing; operational-resilience and third-party rules shape technology architecture; APP reimbursement changes payment economics; Consumer Duty shifts banks toward evidence of good customer outcomes. The PRA’s Basel 3.1 implementation timetable and Strong & Simple framework show capital regulation moving at both systemic-bank and smaller-bank levels. ([Bank of England][10])

**How value moves across the ecosystem:** customers provide deposits, transaction flows and data; banks transform deposits into lending and liquidity; payment infrastructures move money; card networks, processors and open-banking providers capture transaction economics; fraud actors and reimbursement rules transfer value through losses; software, hyperscalers, data, AI, BPO and systems-integrator partners capture transformation and run-cost pools; regulators change permissible conduct, resilience and capital economics.

## 7. Participant Ecosystem Map

```text
Households, SMEs, microbusinesses, corporates, charities
        ↓
Current accounts, savings, cards, mortgages, overdrafts, loans, cash management
        ↓
Bank distribution layer:
apps, branches, contact centres, brokers, Post Office, banking hubs, embedded journeys
        ↓
Bank operating layer:
onboarding, KYC/KYB, servicing, payments, lending, collections, fraud, AML, complaints
        ↓
Decision layer:
credit, pricing, affordability, risk appetite, fraud, financial crime, customer treatment
        ↓
Platform layer:
core banking, ledgers, CRM, data, AI, cloud, APIs, identity, cyber, workflow
        ↓
Infrastructure layer:
RTGS/CHAPS, Faster Payments, Bacs, cards, open banking, LINK, CASS, Confirmation of Payee
        ↓
Regulatory and public-interest layer:
PRA, FCA, PSR, CMA, HMT, FSCS, FOS, ICO, NCA, British Business Bank
```

**Key ecosystem dependencies:**

1. **Current-account primacy remains an industry control point.** It carries salary or business flows, direct debits, customer identity, transaction data and switching friction. The CMA remedies and FCA strategic review both support treating current accounts as a market-shaping product rather than a low-margin utility. ([GOV.UK][13])

2. **Payments infrastructure is a critical dependency.** CHAPS moved £93.9tn across 53.3m payments in 2025; open banking recorded 24bn API calls, 16.5m user connections and 351m open-banking payments in 2025. ([Bank of England][4])

3. **Cash and physical access are being reconstituted through shared infrastructure.** Branch closures, Post Office access and banking hubs show an operating-model shift from proprietary bank premises to shared community access. ([House of Commons Library][11])

4. **Cloud and AI dependencies are now systemic.** The UK’s designation of Microsoft, Google Cloud, AWS and Oracle as critical third parties means major technology suppliers are part of the industry risk perimeter. ([GOV.UK][5])

5. **Fraud is a cross-ecosystem failure mode.** UK Finance reported that 66% of APP cases started online and 17% through telecommunications, showing that banks carry reimbursement and control pressure for harms that often originate outside bank-owned channels. ([UK Finance][9])

## 9. Industry Economics Model

**Economic engine:** deposit-taking banks monetise deposit relationships and lending through net interest income, while carrying credit, liquidity, capital, conduct and operational risk. The current-account relationship is valuable because it gives funding, transaction data, identity, customer primacy and cross-product access. FCA evidence supports the major-bank advantage from personal current accounts and lending/funding scale. ([FCA][2])

**System returns:** the UK system is currently resilient at aggregate level. The Bank of England reported major-bank aggregate CET1 of 14.2%, liquidity coverage ratio of 142% and RoTE of 15.3% in Q1 2026, with higher net interest margins and structural hedge income supporting UK-focused banks. ([Bank of England][6])

**SME economics:** SME finance is shifting toward challengers, specialists and non-bank lenders. British Business Bank data shows challenger/specialist banks accounted for 60% of gross SME bank lending in 2025, while challenger/specialist or non-bank lenders together accounted for 68% of SME lending. This creates a commercial-banking model where incumbent relationship advantage is no longer sufficient by itself. ([British Business Bank][7])

**Cost-to-income dynamics:** incumbents face structural cost from branches, legacy cores, manual controls, regulatory remediation, cyber, resilience and duplicated platforms; challengers face scale, compliance, fraud, funding and profitability pressures. NatWest and Lloyds both publicly frame simplification, application decommissioning, cloud, data and AI as cost, speed and service levers. ([NatWest Group][15])

**Risk economics:** fraud, cyber, APP reimbursement, financial crime, credit impairments, model risk and operational outages are not peripheral losses; they are central to banking economics because they change pricing, controls, customer friction, technology investment and regulatory scrutiny. UK Finance and PSR evidence show APP reimbursement and fraud prevention are now industry operating-model issues. ([UK Finance][9])

**Investment capacity:** strong profitability creates capacity for transformation, but capital, liquidity, regulation, resilience, integration complexity and customer-outcome evidence constrain how that capacity can be used. The strongest economic reinvention mechanisms are therefore simplification, risk-control automation, payments modernisation, AI-assisted service, data quality, cloud resilience and customer-outcome management.

### Most important cross-pressure interactions

1. **Rates + customer resilience + Consumer Duty:** higher or volatile rates affect mortgages, savings and credit stress; banks must show fair treatment, support and affordability discipline rather than only margin protection.

2. **Digital migration + branch closures + inclusion:** branch rationalisation improves cost economics but shifts responsibility to shared hubs, Post Office access, app design and vulnerable-customer servicing.

3. **AI/cloud adoption + operational resilience + CTP oversight:** AI and cloud may improve speed and productivity, but systemic supplier dependency now attracts direct regulatory oversight.

4. **Open banking + Faster Payments + APP fraud:** faster and more embedded payments increase convenience but raise fraud, reimbursement and real-time decisioning pressure.

5. **SME finance + challenger growth + relationship-banking decline:** challenger/specialist lending growth suggests unmet SME needs, but loss of relationship advice can worsen perceived access and application friction.

6. **Profitability + low trust:** strong bank returns can create investment capacity, but customer trust and affordability concerns determine whether transformation is seen as value creation or extraction.

## 15. Unknowns and Contradictions

### Material Unknowns

1. Exact bank-by-bank technology architecture, legacy-core exposure and migration timetable.
2. Supplier workshare, renewal dates, contract values and switching constraints.
3. Independent evidence for claimed AI productivity and customer-service benefits.
4. Bank-level fraud, reimbursement, false-positive and customer-friction economics.
5. How much branch closure harm is mitigated by hubs, Post Office access and digital support.
6. Exact economics of open banking for banks versus fintech and payment participants.
7. How challenger profitability holds through a full credit, fraud and regulatory cycle.
8. UK-specific scope of global technology programmes at HSBC and Santander.
9. Customer willingness to switch primary banking relationship versus using challengers as secondary accounts.
10. Net workforce effect of AI adoption across branches, contact centres, operations, risk and technology.

### Material Contradictions

1. **Digital convenience versus inclusion:** app-led banking improves speed and cost for many customers, while branch and cash reduction can worsen outcomes for cash-dependent, vulnerable or SME customers.

2. **Strong bank profitability versus low trust:** major banks can be well-capitalised and profitable while customer confidence, complaints, fraud and affordability remain unresolved.

3. **Challenger growth versus incumbent advantage:** challengers gain current-account, service and SME-lending relevance, but incumbents retain balance-sheet scale, deposit advantage, capital depth and regulatory experience.

4. **AI productivity versus control burden:** banks report AI benefits, but model governance, explainability, Consumer Duty, resilience and human accountability may absorb part of the productivity gain.

5. **Open banking growth versus fraud and liability:** API and payment growth supports embedded finance, but faster digital money movement increases fraud, reimbursement and real-time decision-control pressure.

6. **Regulatory simplification versus expanding perimeter:** smaller-bank regimes may simplify some prudential burdens, while Consumer Duty, APP reimbursement, CTP oversight and operational resilience expand obligations elsewhere.

## 16. What did Flora learn?

### What is distinctive about Banking

Banking is a trust, licence, ledger, liquidity, capital and payments industry before it is a digital-experience industry. The app is not the enterprise. The enterprise is the controlled movement and transformation of money under capital, conduct, fraud, resilience and customer-outcome constraints.

Deposits are not just a liability. They are funding, customer relationship, transaction data, liquidity input, pricing lever and strategic option. Current accounts remain unusually important because they combine identity, payment flows, data, switching friction and product adjacency.

Risk decisions are production decisions. Credit, fraud, AML, affordability, pricing, reimbursement and customer-treatment decisions create or destroy value just as much as sales and service do.

### Previously observed enterprise Patterns that appear relevant

1. **Channel migration exposes hidden dependency:** digital adoption lowers some costs but reveals dependence on assisted access, shared infrastructure and exception handling.
2. **Legacy simplification funds reinvention:** application decommissioning, cloud migration and data-platform simplification are framed as productivity and investment-capacity levers.
3. **Supplier concentration becomes enterprise risk:** hyperscaler and platform dependencies are now material enough for direct regulatory oversight.
4. **Data/AI value depends on controls:** AI creates value only when decision rights, evidence, controls, explainability and adoption are in place.
5. **Ecosystem participants capture customer moments:** fintechs, payment firms and embedded-finance platforms can own high-value journeys without owning the whole banking balance sheet.

### Patterns that may fail in Banking

1. **“Digital always lowers cost” may fail** because fraud, resilience, compliance, cyber, vulnerability support and dual-channel obligations can absorb savings.
2. **“Better UX wins the bank” may fail** because trust, FSCS protection, salary flows, direct debits, mortgage relationships, credit risk and switching inertia matter.
3. **“AI productivity equals cost takeout” may fail** because human oversight, auditability, complaints, conduct and resilience controls may be mandatory.
4. **“Branch closure means demand disappeared” may fail** because demand can be displaced to Post Office, hubs, carers, contact centres or unserved customer need.
5. **“Open banking disintermediates banks quickly” may fail** because banks still control regulated accounts, deposits, risk and reimbursement obligations.

### New Mechanism candidates

1. **Deposit relationship → structural hedge/NII → investment capacity → simplification/AI/core choices → service and trust.**
2. **APP scam liability → payment-friction design → customer experience → fraud loss and reimbursement economics.**
3. **Cloud/AI concentration → CTP oversight → supplier resilience evidence → architecture and procurement decisions.**
4. **Open-banking API performance → embedded journeys → account primacy pressure → revenue and deposit migration.**
5. **Branch closure → shared access infrastructure → vulnerable/SME customer outcome → regulatory and reputational risk.**
6. **SME workflow data → credit decisioning → working-capital access → challenger/specialist share shift.**

### What Flora should remember permanently

UK banking reinvention must be modelled as a connected system of customer trust, deposits, lending, payments, risk decisions, capital, resilience, technology dependencies and regulation. It is not enough to compare digital features or bank strategies. The durable question is: **which participant controls the customer relationship, the money movement, the risk decision, the regulated obligation and the platform dependency?**

### What must remain uncertain

Supplier superiority, Provider Fit, transformation gaps, future-bank design, exact supplier workshare, private executive intent, proprietary architecture, undisclosed procurement routes, real AI benefit, and bank-specific internal operating performance remain uncertain and must not be inferred from public evidence alone.
