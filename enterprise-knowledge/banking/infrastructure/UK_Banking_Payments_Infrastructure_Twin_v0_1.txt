Requested by:
- User brief: "Researcher Brief — Banking Payments Infrastructure Twin"

Mission:
- Construct the first UK Banking Payments Infrastructure Twin.
- Model the ecosystem that enables UK banking, not one bank.
- Separate what banks control from what the wider payments ecosystem controls.

Scope explicitly included:
- Faster Payments
- Bacs
- CHAPS
- LINK
- Open Banking
- Pay.UK
- Bank of England settlement
- Mastercard
- Visa
- card acquiring
- payment gateways
- Confirmation of Payee
- APP reimbursement
- ISO 20022
- Request to Pay
- Open Banking payments
- merchant acquiring

Boundary:
- United Kingdom banking payments infrastructure.
- Retail and wholesale sterling payments where they affect UK banking mechanisms.
- Account-to-account, bulk, high-value, card, cash-access, open-banking and overlay-service mechanisms.
- Participant behaviour is modelled only where it explains infrastructure dependency.

Not an Enterprise Twin:
- This Twin does not reconstruct a single enterprise's strategy, governance, business-unit economics or commercial opportunity portfolio.
- It adapts the CIOS doctrine into an Industry Infrastructure Twin: infrastructure observations, patterns, mechanisms, dependencies, participants, constraints, unknowns and validation.

Named decisions supported:
D-01 Separate banking mechanisms controlled by banks from mechanisms controlled by shared infrastructure.
D-02 Identify which payment mechanisms are infrastructure-owned, enterprise-owned, shared or externally dependent.
D-03 Determine whether material mechanisms exist around network effects, settlement economics, scheme dependency, fraud propagation, cloud concentration, real-time resilience, third-party dependency, merchant economics and Open Banking competition.
D-04 Prioritise future validation where infrastructure dependency is material but current public evidence is incomplete.

Accountability horizon:
- Near term: July 2026 to September 2026, covering RPIB consultation and current infrastructure modernisation.
- Strategic horizon: 2027 to early 2030s, covering CHAPS extended hours, enhanced ISO 20022, future retail payments infrastructure and incumbent infrastructure contracts.

Source cut-off:
- Sources searched and used up to 18 July 2026.

Materiality rule:
- Include a mechanism when it affects one or more of: payment reachability, settlement finality, liquidity, fraud liability, customer access, merchant economics, scheme compliance, operational resilience, third-party concentration, bank channel design, or Open Banking competition.

Exclusions:
- No proprietary bank data.
- No internal Pay.UK, Bank of England, Mastercard, Visa, LINK, acquirer, gateway or Open Banking operational data.
- No legal advice.
- No commercial opportunity ranking.
- No total programme or market sizing unless directly sourced.

Stop conditions applied:
- Where public evidence supports the existence of a mechanism but not its full economics or ownership, the item is preserved as an Unknown rather than over-claimed.
- Where evidence is provider-authored, supplier-side statements are labelled and not treated as independent proof of benefit.

Refresh triggers:
- RPIB consultation outcome after 11 September 2026.
- Publication of detailed Delivery Company operating model or procurement model.
- Final PSR/FCA directions on Visa/Mastercard scheme and processing fee remedies.
- Further Critical Third Party designations.
- Bank of England/HMT decision on digital pound design phase.
- Material outage or resilience incident in FPS, CHAPS, Bacs, LINK, Visa/Mastercard, major acquirer/gateway or cloud provider.
- APP reimbursement policy or liability changes.
- Open Banking commercial model or VRP regime changes.

2. EXECUTIVE ANSWER
================================================================================

Primary validation answer:
Most high-consequence UK banking mechanisms depend on shared infrastructure rather than enterprise behaviour alone.

A bank owns its customer relationship, interface, risk appetite, authentication design, account ledger, fraud models, operations, compliance interpretation and third-party controls. But a bank does not independently own the reachability, settlement finality, clearing rules, scheme economics, rail availability, network acceptance, cross-bank directory, reimbursement rails, common messaging or central-bank settlement layer that make UK payments work.

The clearest infrastructure-dependent banking mechanisms are:
- cross-bank account-to-account payment reachability;
- 24/7 near-real-time retail transfer availability;
- final settlement in central bank money;
- bulk salary, benefit, bill and Direct Debit processing;
- card acceptance at merchants;
- ATM cash access;
- Confirmation of Payee name checking;
- APP reimbursement claim routing and liability allocation;
- Open Banking payment initiation and VRP execution;
- scheme access and indirect access;
- merchant acquiring economics;
- ISO 20022 data interoperability;
- systemic resilience and cloud concentration management.

The most bank-owned mechanisms are:
- customer authentication and channel experience;
- account ledger management before/after infrastructure handoff;
- customer fraud education and transaction monitoring;
- internal liquidity and treasury posture;
- risk appetite and exception handling;
- bank-specific pricing, products and service design;
- bank-specific third-party due diligence and contingency planning.

The critical control boundary is therefore not "bank versus fintech" or "cards versus account-to-account". It is:
Bank-controlled interaction and account state
-> shared rail and scheme rules
-> central or net settlement
-> overlay trust and liability services
-> external technology and access dependencies
-> customer/merchant behavioural adoption.

Infrastructure model:
- This Twin treats UK payments infrastructure as a layered operating system, not as a list of payment methods.

Layer 0: Public authority and regulatory direction
- HM Treasury
- Bank of England
- FCA
- Payment Systems Regulator
- PVDC
- RPIB
- Future Delivery Company

Layer 1: Settlement foundation
- Bank of England RTGS
- CHAPS
- RTGS settlement accounts, reserves, central bank money
- Net settlement for retail payment systems and card schemes

Layer 2: Core retail interbank rails
- Faster Payments
- Bacs
- Image Clearing System (out of user core scope except Pay.UK context)
- Access routes: direct, indirect, sponsored/agency access

Layer 3: Cash-access rail
- LINK ATM network
- ATM deployers
- card-initiated cash transactions
- cash-access rules and interchange

Layer 4: Card scheme rails
- Visa
- Mastercard
- issuers
- acquirers
- processors
- merchant service providers
- gateways
- tokenisation/wallet providers

Layer 5: Overlay trust and messaging services
- Confirmation of Payee
- Payer Name Verification
- Request to Pay
- APP reimbursement communication and claims-management systems
- Directories and standards

Layer 6: Open Banking rails
- Account-servicing payment service providers (ASPSPs)
- Third-party providers (TPPs)
- Open Banking APIs
- Payment initiation
- Variable Recurring Payments
- Open Banking Limited standards/performance ecosystem

Layer 7: External technology and third-party substrate
- Cloud providers
- managed service providers
- payment processors
- gateways
- scheme connectivity providers
- bureaux
- identity, fraud and device-intelligence services

- Payee verification depends on ecosystem participation, directory accuracy and API availability.

6. INFRASTRUCTURE PATTERNS
================================================================================

PAT-001 Rail reachability pattern
Statement:
- A bank's customer promise to "pay anyone in the UK" is not created by the bank alone. It is produced by sort-code/account addressing, Pay.UK rail participation, direct/indirect access, receiving PSP reachability, and settlement arrangements.
Mechanism relevance:
- Faster Payments, Bacs, CoP, Open Banking PIS, Direct Debit, Direct Credit.
Control state:
- Shared, with infrastructure-owned reachability.

PAT-002 Settlement abstraction pattern
Statement:
- Customers experience payment completion in bank apps or merchant checkouts, but final inter-institution obligation discharge occurs through RTGS gross or net settlement models.
Mechanism relevance:
- CHAPS, FPS, Bacs, LINK, Visa, Mastercard.
Control state:
- Infrastructure-owned settlement; bank-owned customer presentation.

PAT-003 Scheme-rule inheritance pattern
Statement:
- Banks, PSPs, acquirers, gateways and merchants inherit rulebooks, liability rules, data standards and operating requirements from payment systems and card schemes.
Mechanism relevance:
- Card acquiring, FPS APP reimbursement, Bacs Direct Debit, CoP, Open Banking.
Control state:
- Shared; scheme/infrastructure owns rule substrate.

PAT-004 Real-time finality/risk-shift pattern
Statement:
- Faster payment speed improves customer utility but shifts fraud, exception and liquidity stress into shorter decision windows. This creates reliance on preventive overlays such as CoP and after-the-event liability/reimbursement infrastructure.
Mechanism relevance:
- Faster Payments, APP reimbursement, CoP, fraud monitoring.
Control state:
- Shared; risk originates at interaction layer but propagates through rail.

PAT-005 Merchant acceptance two-sided network pattern
Statement:
- Card value depends on consumer credentials, issuer participation, merchant acceptance, acquirer/gateway integration and scheme rules. Account-to-account alternatives must reproduce enough of this acceptance and trust stack to compete.
Mechanism relevance:
- Visa/Mastercard, Open Banking payments, A2A point of sale, merchant acquiring.
Control state:
- Infrastructure-owned network effects with merchant/bank adoption behaviours.

PAT-006 Net settlement efficiency pattern
Statement:
- Retail rails and card schemes use net settlement to reduce liquidity demands versus real-time gross settlement; this creates dependency on prefunding, risk controls, settlement timing and failure management.
Mechanism relevance:
- Bacs, FPS, LINK, Visa, Mastercard.
Control state:
- Infrastructure-owned model; shared participant funding obligations.

PAT-007 Overlay trust pattern
Statement:
- CoP, Payer Name Verification, Request to Pay and APP claims systems add trust, communication or liability primitives above payment rails without becoming the settlement rail themselves.
Mechanism relevance:
- CoP, PNV, RtP, APP reimbursement.
Control state:
- Shared overlay infrastructure.

PAT-008 Indirect-access dependency pattern
Statement:
- Non-bank PSPs and smaller institutions may depend on direct participants or sponsor banks for payment system access, creating operational, commercial and resilience dependence outside their enterprise.
Mechanism relevance:
- FPS, Bacs, CHAPS, Open Banking, acquiring.
Control state:
- Shared/external dependency.

PAT-009 Critical-third-party concentration pattern
Statement:
- As banks, payment firms and FMIs consume common cloud and technology providers, an outage can become system-wide even if each bank's internal controls are adequate.
Mechanism relevance:
- Cloud concentration, operational resilience, gateways, fraud services, Open Banking APIs.
Control state:
- External dependency with regulatory oversight; enterprise controls remain necessary but insufficient.

PAT-010 Future-infrastructure transition pattern
Statement:
- The UK is simultaneously maintaining legacy-critical rails and designing next-generation retail infrastructure. Mechanisms must therefore be modelled as current-state plus transition-state.
Mechanism relevance:
- FPS enhancement, ISO 20022, Certainty of Fate, A2A POS, Open Banking support.
Control state:
- Infrastructure-led, public/private transition.

7. INFRASTRUCTURE MECHANISMS

IM-001 Account-to-account retail payment mechanism
Causal chain:
- Customer/payment initiator -> bank/PSP channel or Open Banking TPP -> account identifiers and authentication -> FPS clearing/messaging -> recipient PSP availability -> net settlement in RTGS -> ledger credit/debit -> confirmation and exception handling.
Rail objects:
- FPS, Open Banking PIS, Pay.UK rules, RTGS net settlement, bank ledgers.
Control:
- Enterprise-owned: customer interface, authentication, account checks, fraud decision, ledger posting.
- Infrastructure-owned: FPS availability, interbank rules, reachability, scheme limits, settlement model.
- Shared: confirmation, exception handling, APP liability.
- External dependency: TPPs, access providers, cloud, fraud vendors.
Validation:
- Infrastructure-dependent.

IM-002 Bulk recurring collection mechanism
Causal chain:
- Biller obtains mandate -> PSP/service user submits Direct Debit -> Bacs processing cycle -> payer bank applies collection -> settlement in RTGS -> Direct Debit guarantee/indemnity and exceptions.
Rail objects:
- Bacs, Direct Debit, Bacs service users, bureaux, payer banks.
Control:
- Enterprise-owned: customer mandate capture quality, account servicing, exception service.
- Infrastructure-owned: Bacs cycle, rules, access, settlement.
- Shared: mandate governance, indemnity, returns.
Validation:
- Infrastructure-dependent.

IM-003 Payroll and benefits disbursement mechanism
Causal chain:
- Employer/government submits file -> Bacs Direct Credit or FPS contingency -> processing cycle -> beneficiary bank credits accounts -> settlement.
Rail objects:
- Bacs Direct Credit, bureaux, Pay.UK, receiving PSPs.
Control:
- Enterprise-owned: file creation, customer account ledger, reconciliation.
- Infrastructure-owned: Bacs clearing and settlement.
- Shared: service-user rules and errors.
Validation:
- Infrastructure-dependent.

IM-004 High-value time-critical payment mechanism
Causal chain:
- Customer/institution initiates CHAPS -> direct/indirect CHAPS participant -> liquidity check -> RTGS gross settlement -> finality -> beneficiary bank credit.
Rail objects:
- CHAPS, RTGS, settlement accounts, intraday liquidity, liquidity saving mechanism.
Control:
- Enterprise-owned: treasury liquidity strategy, customer screening, instructions, operations.
- Infrastructure-owned: CHAPS/RTGS settlement, operating hours, finality rules, liquidity tools.
- Shared: indirect access/agency banking.
Validation:
- Infrastructure-dependent.

IM-005 Card payment acceptance mechanism
Causal chain:
- Cardholder presents credential -> merchant terminal/gateway -> acquirer/processor -> Visa/Mastercard network -> issuer authorisation -> clearing -> net settlement -> merchant funding -> chargeback/dispute flows.
Rail objects:
- Visa, Mastercard, issuers, acquirers, gateways, tokenisation, merchant service agreements.
Control:
- Enterprise-owned: issuer risk model, card proposition, merchant acquirer service model where owned.
- Infrastructure-owned: scheme rules, network routing, acceptance standards, scheme/processing fees.
- Shared: fraud, chargebacks, tokenisation, wallet experience, settlement.
Validation:
- Infrastructure-dependent and scheme-dependent.

IM-006 Cash access mechanism
Causal chain:
- Card/customer authenticates at ATM -> ATM deployer and card network/LINK routing -> issuer authorisation -> cash dispense -> net settlement -> interchange and cash replenishment.
Rail objects:
- LINK, ATMs, issuers, independent ATM deployers, settlement through RTGS.
Control:
- Enterprise-owned: issuer authorisation and customer account.
- Infrastructure-owned: LINK network reach, ATM switching, cash access rules.
- Shared: access footprint, interchange, branch/ATM strategy.
Validation:
- Infrastructure-dependent.

IM-007 Payee verification mechanism
Causal chain:
- Payer enters account details -> payer PSP invokes CoP -> payee PSP responds through API/directory model -> match/no-match result -> payer decision -> payment proceeds or stops.
Rail objects:
- CoP directory, PSP APIs, sort code/account identifiers, Secondary Reference Data.
Control:
- Enterprise-owned: data quality, customer warning design, decisioning.
- Infrastructure-owned: CoP standards, directory, participation rules.
- Shared: customer interpretation and fraud prevention.
Validation:
- Infrastructure-dependent.

IM-008 APP scam reimbursement mechanism
Causal chain:
- Customer reports APP scam -> sending/receiving PSP exchange claim data -> Pay.UK systems support communication and compliance data -> liability apportioned under FPS reimbursement scheme -> reimbursement and reporting.
Rail objects:
- FPS rules, Pay.UK RCMS/core communication/claims systems, PSR directions, PSP operations.
Control:
- Enterprise-owned: case handling, evidence gathering, customer treatment, fraud prevention.
- Infrastructure-owned: scheme rules and operational claims/reporting infrastructure.
- Shared: liability split and prevention incentive.
Validation:
- Infrastructure-dependent.

IM-009 Open Banking payment mechanism
Causal chain:
- Customer consents to TPP -> TPP initiates via ASPSP API -> customer authenticates with ASPSP -> ASPSP initiates payment, commonly over FPS -> confirmation to TPP/customer -> settlement through rail.
Rail objects:
- Open Banking APIs, TPPs, ASPSPs, FPS, VRP consent model.
Control:
- Enterprise-owned: API availability/performance, SCA journey, account risk controls.
- Infrastructure-owned: Open Banking standards, API ecosystem, underlying rail, consent primitives.
- Shared: customer adoption, commercial model, TPP proposition, liability.
Validation:
- Infrastructure-dependent and adoption-dependent.

IM-010 Request-to-Pay bill management mechanism
Causal chain:
- Biller sends secure payment request through RtP-enabled provider -> customer receives request in app/service -> customer pays, part-pays, delays, queries or declines -> payment executed by chosen method -> reconciliation.
Rail objects:
- RtP overlay, billers, PSPs, technical service providers, existing payment methods.
Control:
- Enterprise-owned: biller/customer servicing and channel integration.
- Infrastructure-owned: RtP messaging framework and standards.
- Shared: adoption, integration and reconciliation value.
Validation:
- Overlay-infrastructure-dependent; not settlement-rail-owned.

IM-011 Merchant economics mechanism
Causal chain:
- Merchant chooses acceptance methods -> gateway/acquirer prices service -> card scheme and processing fees/interchange flow through -> merchant service charge and settlement timing affect margin -> alternative A2A/Open Banking methods compete only if accepted/trusted.
Rail objects:
- Visa/Mastercard, acquirers, gateways, Open Banking payments, A2A POS future infrastructure.
Control:
- Enterprise-owned: merchant payment strategy and bank/acquirer proposition.
- Infrastructure-owned: scheme fees, network acceptance, rule economics.
- Shared: acquirer competition, gateway value, customer behaviour.
Validation:
- Infrastructure and scheme economics dominate.

IM-012 ISO 20022 enhanced-data mechanism
Causal chain:
- Sender creates structured payment message -> bank/PSP systems capture and validate data -> rail carries ISO 20022 payload -> recipient systems consume data -> reconciliation, compliance, analytics and automation improve if data quality is adopted.
Rail objects:
- CHAPS/RTGS ISO 20022, future Pay.UK ISO 20022, mapping libraries, bank core systems.
Control:
- Enterprise-owned: data capture, systems changes, operating adoption.
- Infrastructure-owned: standard, message rules and mandatory fields.
- Shared: network-wide adoption and end-to-end data quality.
Validation:
- Shared; infrastructure sets the language, banks must use it.

IM-013 Cloud and critical-third-party resilience mechanism
Causal chain:
- Bank/PSP/FMI workloads depend on common technology providers -> outage or cyber incident disrupts multiple firms or markets -> regulators oversee CTPs -> firms remain responsible for own outsourcing and resilience controls.
Rail objects:
- AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft, Oracle, regulators, bank systems, FMI systems.
Control:
- Enterprise-owned: architecture, exit plans, due diligence, contingency.
- Infrastructure/external-owned: provider service resilience, shared platforms, regulatory CTP oversight.
- Shared: incident communication, testing, recovery.
Validation:
- External dependency with shared control.

9. MECHANISM VALIDATION: REQUIRED THEMES
================================================================================

VM-001 Payment network effects
Validation: EXISTS.
Evidence:
- Card payments represent a dominant share of UK payment transactions (S-070). Visa and Mastercard operate global networks with issuers/acquirers and large payment volumes (S-060, S-061, S-062). Open Banking payments are growing but remain far smaller than total UK payments (S-040, S-070).
Mechanism:
- Value increases with the number of usable credentials, accepting merchants, issuers/acquirers, TPP integrations and customer trust.
Control allocation:
- Infrastructure owned: scheme acceptance rules, network routing, standards.
- Shared: issuer/acquirer participation, merchant acceptance, consumer adoption.
- Enterprise owned: product design, channel friction, incentives.
Judgement:
- Strong mechanism. Cards have mature network effects; Open Banking is developing early network effects but still lacks equivalent universal acceptance and consumer recourse.

VM-002 Settlement economics
Validation: EXISTS.
Evidence:
- CHAPS gross settlement eliminates settlement risk but requires liquidity (S-022). RTGS supports central-bank-money settlement and settlement across CHAPS and retail systems (S-021, S-022). RTGS settles over £800bn average working day (S-020).
Mechanism:
- Infrastructure chooses gross versus net settlement; banks manage liquidity, prefunding and collateral inside that model.
Control allocation:
- Infrastructure owned: settlement model, RTGS account design, operating hours, finality.
- Enterprise owned: treasury operations, funding, collateral optimisation.
Judgement:
- Strong mechanism. Settlement economics are infrastructure-owned at model level and bank-owned at position-management level.

VM-003 Scheme dependency
Validation: EXISTS.
Evidence:
- Pay.UK operates core retail rails and overlay services (S-010). Bank of England operates settlement/CHAPS (S-021, S-022). Visa/Mastercard do not face effective competition in scheme/processing fees according to PSR (S-031).
Mechanism:
- Banks and payment firms inherit scheme rules, access requirements, settlement windows, fee structures and liability arrangements.
Control allocation:
- Infrastructure owned: rulebooks, scheme fees, access models.
- Shared: compliance, operational execution.
Judgement:
- Strong mechanism. Banking payments are rule-inherited, not enterprise-autonomous.

VM-004 Fraud propagation
Validation: EXISTS.
Evidence:
- APP reimbursement applies to firms using FPS and is built into FPS rules and Pay.UK operational systems (S-030). CoP performs account name checking and processes more than 2m checks daily (S-014).
Mechanism:
- Real-time payment rails compress fraud decision windows; fraud losses propagate through sending and receiving PSPs, mule accounts, customer deception and rail speed.
Control allocation:
- Enterprise owned: monitoring, warnings, holds, customer service.
- Infrastructure owned: FPS reimbursement rules, CoP service, claims communication infrastructure.
- External dependency: social media, telecoms, device intelligence, mule networks.
Judgement:
- Strong mechanism. Fraud is not merely an enterprise behaviour problem; it is shaped by rail speed, shared identity/name-checking services and liability infrastructure.

VM-005 Cloud concentration
Validation: EXISTS.
Evidence:
- HMT designated AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft and Oracle as Critical Third Parties; Bank/PRA/FCA oversight began on 13 July 2026 (S-080, S-081).
Mechanism:
- Common technology providers can become systemic failure points for banks, PSPs and FMIs.
Control allocation:
- External dependency: provider infrastructure.
- Enterprise owned: resilience architecture and vendor risk.
- Regulatory/infrastructure oversight: CTP regime.
Judgement:
- Strong mechanism. Cloud concentration is now explicitly recognised as a system-level financial-infrastructure dependency.

VM-006 Real-time payment resilience
Validation: EXISTS.
Evidence:
- FPS is 24/7/365 (S-011). RPIB consultation aims at next-generation infrastructure that remains resilient and trusted while supporting innovation (S-025). Pay.UK is enhancing resilience including Certainty of Fate (S-016).
Mechanism:
- The more banking depends on immediate A2A transfer, the more rail availability, confirmation, exception handling and contingency become critical.
Control allocation:
- Infrastructure owned: rail availability, rules, central processing, future design.
- Shared: bank connectivity, incident response, customer communication.
Judgement:
- Strong mechanism. Real-time resilience is a shared system property, not a single-bank feature.

VM-007 Third-party dependency
Validation: EXISTS.
Evidence:
- Pay.UK direct/indirect access routes depend on direct participants/IAPs for indirect access (S-013). Vocalink/Pay.UK sources show central infrastructure supplier roles for FPS/Bacs/ICS (S-090, S-091, S-092). CTP regime identifies cloud provider dependency (S-080, S-081).
Mechanism:
- Participants often rely on sponsor banks, bureaux, processors, connectivity providers, gateways and cloud.
Control allocation:
- Enterprise owned: due diligence, contractual controls, contingency.
- External dependency: provider performance and concentration.
- Infrastructure owned: access and assurance rules.
Judgement:
- Strong mechanism. Third-party dependency is structurally embedded.

VM-008 Merchant economics
Validation: EXISTS.
Evidence:
- PSR found Mastercard/Visa scheme and processing fees lack effective competition and clarity (S-031). PSR found card acquiring did not work well for merchants with annual card turnover below £50m (S-032). UK Finance shows cards remain 64% of transactions (S-070).
Mechanism:
- Merchant costs and payment-method choices depend on scheme fees, interchange, acquirer pricing, gateway services, settlement timing, chargebacks and consumer preference.
Control allocation:
- Infrastructure owned: scheme fee/processing economics.
- Shared: acquirer competition, merchant checkout choice.
- Enterprise owned: merchant contract negotiation and steering.
Judgement:
- Strong mechanism. Merchant economics are infrastructure and scheme controlled more than bank controlled.

VM-009 Open Banking competition
Validation: EXISTS, but maturity is still emerging.
Evidence:
- OBL reports 351m Open Banking payments in 2025, 57% growth and 16.5m user connections (S-040). FCA reports 53% year-on-year payment growth and VRPs at 16% of Open Banking transactions (S-041). RPIB future infrastructure may support A2A point-of-sale (S-025). UK Finance shows 48.8bn UK payments and card payments at 64% share (S-070).
Mechanism:
- Open Banking competes by using bank APIs and A2A rails to bypass card economics, but must solve acceptance, recourse, trust, refund/dispute handling, consumer habit and merchant integration.
Control allocation:
- Infrastructure owned: API standards, FPS/A2A rails, future A2A POS infrastructure.
- Shared: ASPSP implementation, TPP innovation, merchant acceptance, customer trust.
Judgement:
- Real but incomplete. Open Banking is a competitive pressure mechanism, not yet a dominant replacement mechanism.

10. DEPENDENCY CHAINS BY PAYMENT DOMAIN
================================================================================

10.1 Faster Payments chain
Mechanism:
- Instant bank-account transfer.
Dependency chain:
- Customer/account holder
-> bank channel or Open Banking TPP
-> authentication and fraud checks
-> sort code/account reachability
-> FPS rule and central processing
-> receiving PSP availability
-> net settlement through RTGS
-> confirmation/exception handling
-> APP reimbursement if scam occurs.

Control separation:
- Bank controls interaction, authentication and account ledger.
- Pay.UK/FPS controls central rail rules and processing.
- Bank of England controls central-bank settlement.
- PSR/Pay.UK control reimbursement rule embedding.
- External providers may control cloud, fraud tooling, connectivity and TPP access.

Infrastructure dependency:
- Critical.

10.2 Bacs chain
Mechanism:
- Bulk recurring payments and collections.
Dependency chain:
- Employer/biller/service user
-> bureau or direct submission
-> Bacs rules and processing cycle
-> payer/beneficiary bank processing
-> net settlement through RTGS
-> exception, return, indemnity or reconciliation flow.

Control separation:
- Enterprise controls mandate/file quality.
- Pay.UK/Bacs controls scheme and processing.
- Bank of England RTGS provides settlement.
- Bureaux and FM providers may mediate access.

Infrastructure dependency:
- Critical.

10.3 CHAPS/RTGS chain
Mechanism:
- High-value same-day, final sterling payment.
Dependency chain:
- Customer/institution instruction
-> CHAPS participant or agency bank
-> liquidity and compliance controls
-> RTGS gross settlement in central bank money
-> beneficiary credit and finality.

Control separation:
- Bank controls instruction validation and liquidity position.
- Bank of England controls CHAPS/RTGS infrastructure, hours, finality and liquidity tools.

Infrastructure dependency:
- Critical.

10.4 LINK cash-access chain
Mechanism:
- Cash withdrawal away from own bank branch.
Dependency chain:
- Cardholder
-> ATM and deployer
-> LINK/network routing
-> issuer authorisation
-> dispense
-> settlement/interchange/cash logistics.

Control separation:
- Bank controls issuer authorisation.
- LINK/network controls shared access.
- ATM deployers and cash logistics create external dependency.

Infrastructure dependency:
- Critical.

10.5 Card acquiring and gateway chain
Mechanism:
- Merchant accepts cards in store or online.
Dependency chain:
- Merchant checkout
-> terminal/gateway
-> acquirer/processor
-> Visa/Mastercard network
-> issuer authorisation
-> clearing and net settlement
-> merchant funding
-> disputes/chargebacks.

Control separation:
- Issuer controls customer risk and cardholder relationship.
- Acquirer controls merchant relationship.
- Gateway controls merchant technical route.
- Visa/Mastercard control scheme rules and economics.
- Bank of England supports net settlement for Visa Europe/Mastercard Europe obligations through RTGS.

Infrastructure dependency:
- Critical.

10.6 Open Banking payment chain
Mechanism:
- Bank API-enabled account-to-account payment.
Dependency chain:
- Customer consents to TPP
-> TPP calls ASPSP API
-> ASPSP authenticates and authorises
-> underlying payment rail executes, often FPS
-> confirmation to TPP/customer
-> settlement and reconciliation.

Control separation:
- ASPSP controls API availability and SCA journey.
- Open Banking standards and TPP ecosystem control interoperability.
- FPS/RTGS controls actual payment execution and settlement.
- TPP controls proposition and customer experience.

Infrastructure dependency:
- High and shared.

11. INFRASTRUCTURE CONSTRAINTS
================================================================================

IC-001 Shared rail rule constraint
Description:
- Banks cannot unilaterally change cross-bank payment rail rules.
Affected mechanisms:
- FPS, Bacs, CoP, APP reimbursement, Open Banking payment initiation, RtP.
Consequence:
- Innovation, fraud controls and customer experience must work within common rules.

IC-002 Settlement finality constraint
Description:
- Final inter-institution settlement depends on RTGS and central bank money, not bank preference.
Affected mechanisms:
- CHAPS, FPS, Bacs, LINK, Visa, Mastercard.
Consequence:
- Liquidity, timing and risk management are bounded by settlement infrastructure.

IC-003 Legacy-to-next-generation transition constraint
Description:
- Existing rails must remain resilient while next-generation infrastructure is designed and delivered.
Affected mechanisms:
- FPS, Bacs, Open Banking, A2A POS, ISO 20022.
Consequence:
- Banks and infrastructure operators must invest in both continuity and transformation.

IC-004 Scheme economics constraint
Description:
- Visa/Mastercard scheme and processing fees are set by schemes and challenged by PSR due to competition concerns.
Affected mechanisms:
- Card acquiring, merchant economics, A2A competition.
Consequence:
- Merchant costs and competitive pressure depend on regulatory and scheme changes.

IC-005 Consumer trust/recourse constraint
Description:
- Cards have mature dispute/chargeback expectations; bank transfers historically differed, and APP reimbursement partially changes the recourse profile.
Affected mechanisms:
- Cards, Open Banking, FPS, APP reimbursement.
Consequence:
- A2A/Open Banking competition must solve trust and recourse, not only price.

IC-006 Fraud-speed constraint
Description:
- Real-time payment speed reduces the time available for fraud detection, confirmation and intervention.
Affected mechanisms:
- FPS, Open Banking PIS, CoP, APP reimbursement.
Consequence:
- Prevention must move upstream; after-the-event reimbursement changes incentives but does not eliminate fraud.

IC-007 Cloud/third-party concentration constraint
Description:
- Multiple firms and infrastructure providers may rely on common technology providers.
Affected mechanisms:
- API availability, bank channels, gateways, rail operations, fraud tooling.
Consequence:
- Single-provider failures can become multi-firm incidents.

IC-008 Indirect-access constraint
Description:
- Indirect PSPs depend on sponsor banks/direct participants for reachability.
Affected mechanisms:
- FPS, Bacs, CHAPS, Open Banking payment execution.
Consequence:
- Competition and resilience may depend on indirect access quality and sponsor relationships.

IC-009 Data-quality constraint
Description:
- ISO 20022 and CoP benefits require accurate, structured data and adoption across many parties.
Affected mechanisms:
- CHAPS, future retail ISO 20022, CoP, PNV, fraud analytics.
Consequence:
- Infrastructure mandates are necessary but insufficient without bank/process adoption.

IC-010 Merchant adoption constraint
Description:
- A2A and Open Banking payments must compete against cards' installed acceptance base, consumer familiarity and recourse.
Affected mechanisms:
- Open Banking payments, A2A POS, merchant acquiring.
Consequence:
- Competition will be constrained unless merchant incentives and customer trust align.

15. ANSWER TO "WHICH BANKING MECHANISMS DEPEND UPON INFRASTRUCTURE RATHER THAN ENTERPRISE BEHAVIOUR?"
================================================================================

Direct answer:
The following mechanisms depend primarily on infrastructure, although enterprise behaviour affects performance at the edge:

- Settlement finality: infrastructure-owned through Bank of England RTGS and central bank money.
- CHAPS high-value final payment: infrastructure-owned through CHAPS/RTGS; bank-owned liquidity decisions sit inside that system.
- Faster Payments reachability and 24/7 operation: infrastructure-owned through FPS/Pay.UK and central infrastructure.
- Bacs Direct Debit and Direct Credit processing: infrastructure-owned through Pay.UK/Bacs, with bank/biller operational obligations.
- Card acceptance and scheme economics: infrastructure-owned through Visa/Mastercard rules, network effects and scheme fees; acquirers/gateways intermediate merchant access.
- LINK cash access: infrastructure-owned network reach, with bank issuer authorisation and cash-estate choices.
- Confirmation of Payee: shared overlay; no bank can provide universal name checking without ecosystem API/directory participation.
- APP reimbursement: shared liability and rule infrastructure embedded into FPS; banks execute claims but do not unilaterally define the regime.
- Open Banking payments: shared API/standards/FPS dependency; banks expose APIs and authenticate customers but do not own the whole payment mechanism.
- ISO 20022: infrastructure-owned standards adoption; banks realise value only if they capture/use structured data.
- Merchant acquiring economics: scheme/acquirer/gateway infrastructure dominates cost and acceptance; merchants and banks can optimise but not fully control.
- Cloud and critical third-party resilience: external infrastructure dependency with regulatory oversight; enterprise controls are necessary but insufficient.

Mechanisms that are more enterprise-owned but still infrastructure-constrained:
- customer authentication;
- payment warning and user-experience design;
- internal fraud models;
- treasury liquidity positioning;
- ledger posting and reconciliation;
- complaint handling;
- product pricing and merchant steering;
- supplier due diligence.

Therefore:
- The bank owns "how it participates".
- The infrastructure owns "what is interoperable, settled, reachable, trusted, and economically possible across the market".
- The customer and merchant experience emerges from both.
