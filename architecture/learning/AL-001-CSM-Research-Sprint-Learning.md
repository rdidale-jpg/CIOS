# AL-001 — CSM Research Sprint Learning

**Document class:** Architecture learning record  
**Status:** Review  
**Owner:** Rob / CIOS  
**Last updated:** 2026-07-14  
**Production behaviour:** Documentation-only architecture learning. Does not change runtime behaviour, production Researcher packs, Enterprise Model, Observation Model, Knowledge Graph, AP-001 or AP-002.  
**Promotion note:** This record remains Review architecture until validated across materially different enterprises.

## Purpose

Capture architectural learning from the completed CSM Public-Domain Research Sprint and convert the learning into the next generation of Enterprise Intelligence Research methodology.

This record is architecture only. It does not promote sprint outputs, Review material or opportunity claims into Accepted architecture or canonical enterprise truth.

## Original problem

The initial Research Sprint anchored on the wrong procurement object.

The Researcher treated an available procurement as if it were the commercial opportunity to research. That produced a plausible public-domain research path, but it did not begin with an enterprise-wide understanding of CSM's transformation portfolio, programme landscape or opportunity landscape.

The result was commercially less useful than it should have been because the research object was narrower than the enterprise opportunity space.

## Why the wrong procurement anchor occurred

The sprint implicitly assumed that the opportunity object had already been identified before research began. The Researcher therefore optimised for evidence collection around the known or visible procurement rather than asking whether that procurement was the right commercial object.

The failure mode was not primarily poor source work. It was an architectural gap: CIOS had not yet defined Enterprise Opportunity Discovery as a mandatory upstream stage.

## Root cause

Research assumed the opportunity object had already been identified.

In reality, Enterprise Opportunity Discovery had never been defined. There was no standard method requiring the Researcher to identify all commercially significant transformation opportunities before selecting one for a full Research Sprint.

The methodology therefore lacked a formal distinction between:

- researching an enterprise;
- identifying its transformation opportunity portfolio;
- selecting a research object;
- validating that research object;
- researching the selected opportunity;
- converting research into positioning intelligence.

## Architectural improvements learned

### Research Object Validation

Research Object Validation must confirm that the selected opportunity object is the right object before deep research proceeds. It should test whether the apparent procurement is:

- the opportunity itself;
- evidence of a wider programme;
- a component of a multi-phase transformation;
- an implementation route within a broader partner model;
- a misleading or stale artefact;
- insufficiently evidenced for commercial pursuit.

### Research-to-Positioning Contract

Research must hand over to positioning through an explicit contract. The contract should state:

- what is evidenced;
- what is inferred;
- what remains unknown;
- what contradicts the current view;
- what commercial questions positioning may answer;
- what positioning must not claim.

### Opportunity Positioning Intelligence

The sprint showed that commercial usefulness improved materially when research outputs were translated into opportunity positioning intelligence rather than left as enterprise description.

Positioning intelligence should describe the opportunity's strategic relevance, executive problem, likely decision frame, competitive context, supplier implications and evidence-governed pursuit implications.

### Positioning Insight Deepening

A first positioning pass is not sufficient. The sprint improved when positioning was deepened into clearer theses, narratives, proof requirements and conversation plays while preserving evidence boundaries.

### Three Strategic Thesis model

The methodology should support multiple competing or complementary strategic theses rather than forcing a single premature conclusion. The model allows the team to compare plausible commercial interpretations and preserve uncertainty.

### Executive Narratives

Executive Narratives translate evidence-governed understanding into concise, executive-relevant stories. They must remain grounded in public-domain evidence and explicitly mark assumptions, caveats and unknowns.

### Proof Architecture

Proof Architecture defines what evidence would be needed to make a positioning claim credible. It separates persuasive narrative from the proof required to support it.

### Conversation Plays

Conversation Plays convert positioning into practical executive conversation paths. They must be bounded by the Research-to-Positioning Contract and must not introduce unsupported provider fit or private account knowledge.

### Decision Envelope improvements

The Decision Envelope must distinguish:

- **Supported** — public evidence supports the conclusion sufficiently for the stated use.
- **Supported with Caveats** — evidence supports the conclusion but material unknowns, dependencies, contradictions or freshness issues remain.
- **Not Supported** — evidence does not support the conclusion or the conclusion would require unsupported inference.

The improved Decision Envelope should include evidence demand, confidence, freshness, contradictions and recommended next validation steps.

## Confirmed improvements

The CSM sprint confirmed that the following improvements increased commercial usefulness within the reviewed sprint context:

- validating the research object before deepening research;
- separating enterprise understanding from opportunity positioning;
- adding a Research-to-Positioning Contract;
- preserving unknowns and contradictions;
- treating procurements as evidence of change rather than as the definition of the opportunity;
- using multiple strategic theses;
- developing executive narratives and conversation plays from evidence-governed research;
- making proof requirements explicit before pursuit claims are made.

## Remaining Unknowns

The sprint does not yet prove that the improved methodology generalises across all enterprise types, sectors, procurement regimes or transformation patterns.

Remaining unknowns include:

- how much public evidence is sufficient for Enterprise Opportunity Discovery in low-transparency sectors;
- how the method performs where procurement evidence is fragmented, delayed or deliberately opaque;
- how to calibrate opportunity maturity consistently across industries;
- how to identify emerging opportunities without over-inferring future procurements;
- how to compare opportunities where evidence confidence and commercial accessibility diverge.

## Items still requiring validation

Before promotion beyond Review, the architecture should be validated against materially different enterprises, including at least:

- a private-sector enterprise with limited public procurement evidence;
- a public-sector enterprise with many overlapping procurement frameworks;
- a regulated infrastructure or utilities enterprise;
- a fast-moving digital or technology enterprise;
- an enterprise where the apparent procurement is not the strategic opportunity.

Validation should test whether Enterprise Opportunity Discovery identifies the right opportunity portfolio, whether Research Object Validation prevents false anchoring and whether the Research-to-Positioning Contract preserves evidence governance.

## Recommendation

The CSM learning should be captured as Review architecture and used to guide further validation.

Research Object Validation, Research-to-Positioning Contract, Opportunity Positioning Intelligence, Positioning Insight Deepening and Enterprise Opportunity Discovery should remain Review architecture until validated across materially different enterprises. They must not be treated as Accepted architecture, promoted into production Researcher packs or used to change runtime behaviour without the appropriate governance path.
